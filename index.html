What Is a Micro:bit?

A tiny computer that fits in your palm.

Each micro:bit has buttons, a light sensor, and can feel when you tilt or shake it. Today, every one of them is wirelessly connected to this room, so the moment you move yours, something on screen moves too.

How to Join In

Pick a station. Try a move. Share the mic.

Step up to any station and grab a labeled micro:bit. Try pressing Button A, tilting left or right, or shaking it. Listen and look for your change on the big screen — then pass it to the next person in line.

For Grown-ups & Teachers

Physical computing, made welcoming.

This exhibit shows how a single radio hub can turn a roomful of micro:bits into a shared creative instrument. The student firmware is an open MakeCode project you can open, fork, and flash to take back to your classroom, club, or library.

What You're Actually Doing

You're writing a little music for the real world.

"Physical computing" just means you make the computer react to something physical — a button, a tilt, a shake. Here's how today's station pulls it off.

A

Your micro:bit senses you.

When you press a button or tilt the board, tiny sensors inside the micro:bit notice what just happened — the same way your phone knows which way you're holding it.

B

It whispers over radio.

Your micro:bit quietly sends a short message through the air to a "hub" micro:bit plugged into our laptop. One hub listens to the whole room at once.

C

The room reacts together.

The laptop turns those messages into music notes, moving characters, or new lines on a drawing — all live, all shared. The more people play, the wilder it gets.
