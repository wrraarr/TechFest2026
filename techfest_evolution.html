The project changed because people walked up and talked to us.

This page captures the evolution of the Micro:bit Exploration project during Luddy TechFest on Saturday, April 11, 2026. Visitors tested things, noticed rough spots, pitched upgrades, and helped shape what the code became by the end of the day.

Arvin, our 8-year-old helper, wrote down update ideas as people shared them. One of the biggest additions from that live feedback loop was the Zombie game.

What Changed The Work

Real people gave us real-time design review: make this bigger, make that clearer, add a game, add explosions, make the canvas fill the screen, and let the characters do more.

Arvin's Contribution

Arvin helped us capture the stream of ideas during the day, and his idea pushed us to build the Zombie Outbreak page as a new game inside the project.

Why This Page Exists

It shows that the code was not fixed ahead of time. It evolved in public, with comments and imagination from TechFest participants guiding the next changes.

Evolution Timeline

Updates shaped by TechFest feedback

The sequence below reflects the kinds of changes we made during Saturday, April 11, 2026 as people interacted with the stations and suggested new directions.

01

The activity got clearer

Visitors wanted the home page to better explain what participants were actually doing, so we expanded the activity framing with more cards about noticing errors, improving ideas, and making new pages or controllers.

Better explanation More welcoming entry point Visitor-driven wording
02

The drawing canvas became bigger and more immersive

People wanted the drawing experience to feel larger, so the motion canvas was pushed toward full-width and taller proportions. That made the sketch station work better as a shared public screen instead of a small widget inside a page.

Full-screen feel Projector-friendly Shared canvas energy
03

The character world turned into a game space

Feedback pushed the character page from a motion demo into a playful arena. Characters got shooting, visible aiming directions, explosions when hit, respawning behavior, and a full-screen beach environment that felt more like a world than a graph.

Explosions Aiming controls Beach world
04

Randomness made the action more alive

When people noticed characters were coming back in a predictable way, we changed respawn behavior to feel less centered and more playful. That made the field feel less staged and more dynamic for the next group stepping up.

Random respawn Less predictable More playful chaos
05

Arvin's idea became Zombie Outbreak

One of the biggest leaps of the day came from Arvin's idea: turn the node system into a zombie game. We built a new page where one person can be infected, zombies spread by touch, survivors can shoot, and the project's micro:bit communication protocol still drives the action.

Arvin's idea New game page Same micro:bit protocol
06

The zombie game kept evolving with visitors

Once the zombie idea landed, people kept adding to it. Zombies became player-controlled, a weak random zombie bot was added, and safe-house buildings created ten-second windows of protection. The game became a live example of rapid iteration in response to audience imagination.

Player-controlled zombies Slow bot zombie Safe houses

Who Shaped It

The code evolved because the room did

These were not abstract feature requests. They came from children, families, helpers, and visitors reacting to what they saw on the screen and imagining what it could become next.

Visitors

Visitors tested the station in the most honest possible way: by trying it immediately and saying what would make it more fun, more readable, bigger, clearer, or more game-like.

Arvin

Arvin, our 8-year-old helper, helped capture the update ideas during the day and contributed the idea that became the Zombie Outbreak page.

The Build Process

The project became a living studio. Observe what people do, listen to what they suggest, make the change, and let the next person react to the new version.
